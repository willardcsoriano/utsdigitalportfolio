My Cultural Self

Connecting to traditions and beliefs.

I grew up in Marikina City, a place known for its rich culture of craftsmanship and history. My parents, both from the northern part of Luzon, spoke Ilocano, connecting them to a distinct cultural heritage. Although I learned to speak it as well, my proficiency remains limited, which perhaps symbolizes my broader disconnect from these roots.

This feeling of detachment can be understood through the lens of the ecological systems theory. The microsystem, which includes the immediate environments such as family and neighborhood, has a profound influence on personal development. Despite the rich cultural background provided by my parents' heritage, the dominant cultural environment of Marikina, where I was raised, played a more significant role in shaping my identity. This geographic influence highlights the complex interplay between various elements of the microsystem in shaping one's cultural self.

Furthermore, the ‘cultural self’ emphasizes that our development is influenced by crucial social groups, particularly family and friends, which act as the most pervasive and influential social groups over the course of our development. In my case, while my family did provide a link to the Ilocano culture, the stronger cultural forces of the city where I lived seemed to overshadow this connection, leading me to identify more with Marikina than with the ethnic origins of my family.

In reflecting on this, I see the importance of both geographic and familial influences in shaping my cultural identity. Individual identity arises from a complex interplay of factors within one's microsystem, not from a single source. Through this understanding, I appreciate the role that both place and heritage play in defining who I am.
